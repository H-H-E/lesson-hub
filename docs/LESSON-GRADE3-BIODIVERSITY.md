# Grade 3 Science: Biodiversity
## A Systems Literacy Approach

**Grade Level:** 3  
**Subject:** Science  
**Duration:** 60 minutes (can be split across 2 sessions)  
**Theme:** Understanding Interdependence — "You are not a lone creature..."

---

> *"You are not a lone creature being bossed around by morality; you are a living process inside other living processes."* — Systems Literacy Vision

---

## 1. SMART Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. **Define** biodiversity as "the variety of all living things—plants, animals, and microorganisms—in a place" with 90% accuracy.

2. **Identify** at least 3 reasons why biodiversity matters (provides food, cleans air/water, keeps ecosystems healthy) using evidence from the lesson.

3. **Classify** living things into categories (producers, consumers, decomposers) and explain how each category helps the ecosystem survive.

4. **Analyze** how removing one species affects other species in an ecosystem, demonstrating systems thinking.

5. **Design** a plan to increase biodiversity in their schoolyard or local community.

## 3. Hook (5-7 minutes)

### "The Empty Plate"

**Setup:** Before students enter, set up a "picnic" display with pictures of different foods (fruits, vegetables, bread, cheese, meat). Cover with a sheet.

**Script:**
> "Class, I have a very special picnic here. But here's the problem—I need YOUR help to figure something out. Imagine all of these foods... and then imagine they all DISAPPEARED. What would you eat?"

**Engagement:** Have students brainstorm. Write responses on the board.

**The Twist:**
> "Now here's the science question: Why do we have so many different foods? Where do they come from? And why does it matter that we have LOTS of different kinds—not just one kind of fruit or one kind of vegetable?"

**Reveal:**
> "Today we're going to discover why having LOTS of different living things—plants, animals, bugs, even tiny microbes—is one of the most important things on Earth. Scientists have a word for this: BIODIVERSITY. And you're about to find out why it's SO important that scientists worry about losing it."

**Transition:** "Let's become biodiversity scientists!"

## 4. Lesson Sequence

### Part 1: Direct Instruction (12 minutes)

**A. Introduce Biodiversity**
- Display word on board: BIODIVERSITY
- Break it down: "Bio" = living things, "Diversity" = differences
- Child-friendly definition: "All the different kinds of life in a place"

**B. The Biodiversity Map**
> "Let's see how many different places on Earth have biodiversity. I'll name a place—you tell me what living things might live there!"

| Place | Examples to Add |
|-------|-----------------|
| Rainforest | Monkeys, jaguars, orchids, frogs, insects |
| Ocean | Fish, whales, coral, seaweed, sharks |
| Desert | Camels, cacti, lizards, scorpions, beetles |
| Your backyard | Birds, squirrels, worms, flowers, ants |

**C. Systems Connection — The Key Insight**
> "Here's something amazing: ALL these living things are CONNECTED. They're not just hanging out together—they NEED each other! It's like a giant team where everyone has a job."

**D. Introduce the Three Jobs (Classification)**

| Role | Job | Examples |
|------|-----|----------|
| **Producers** | Make food (using sunlight!) | Plants, algae |
| **Consumers** | Eat other living things | Animals, humans |
| **Decomposers** | Break down dead things | Fungi, bacteria, worms |

**Memory Trick:** "Producers PRODUCE food. Consumers CONSUME (eat) food. Decomposers DECOMPOSE (break down) dead things."

---

### Part 2: Guided Practice — The Food Web Activity (15 minutes)

**Setup:** Each student or pair gets a classification card with a living thing.

**A. Create the Living Chain (10 min)**
- Students stand in a circle
- Pass out yarn ball to a "producer" (plant)
- They hold the yarn and pass to someone they "feed"
- Continue until everyone is connected
- Result: A giant web!

**B. The "Remove One" Experiment (5 min)**
> "Imagine this: A disease kills all the grass (producer). What happens?"

- Have the "grass" students sit down
- Let yarn slack — show connected students losing their "food source"
- Discuss: What happens to the rabbits? The hawks? The soil?

**Key Question:**
> "If we remove just ONE kind of living thing, the whole web gets weaker. That's why biodiversity is like a SAFETY NET for nature!"

---

### Part 3: Collaborative Investigation (15 minutes)

**A. Ecosystem Stations (Rotating, 4 stations, ~3-4 min each)**

| Station | Activity | Materials |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| **1. Sort It Out** | Classify cards into producers/consumers/decomposers | Sort cards, recording sheet |
| **2. Build a Food Web** | Draw arrows connecting who eats what in a pond | Pond ecosystem sheet, markers |
| **3. Why Does It Matter?** | Read scenario cards and predict what happens | Scenario cards |
| **4. Design for Life** | Plan how to add MORE biodiversity to schoolyard | Design worksheet |

**B. Station Debrief (5 min)**
- Each group shares one insight
- Chart common themes: "variety = resilience," "everything is connected," "small changes matter"

---

### Part 4: Synthesis & Closing (8 minutes)

**A. The Big Picture**
> "Remember: You are NOT a lone creature. Every breath you take, every bite you eat, every step you take—they all connect you to other living things. Biodiversity isn't just about 'saving the environment.' It's about understanding that YOU are part of the team."

**B. Exit Ticket (3 min)**
Quick check: "Name one thing you learned about biodiversity today and one action you could take to help."

## 5. Three-Level Differentiation

### Support (Level 1: Approaching)

- **Visual Aids:** Provide picture cards with labels for classification
- **Sentence Stems:** "A producer is..." "A consumer eats..." "Biodiversity means..."
- **Simplified Sort:** Limit to 6 cards instead of 12
- **Paired Work:** Partner with an advanced learner
- **Vocabulary Preview:** Pre-teach key words before lesson
- **Graphic Organizer:** Provide partially completed food web

### On-Level (Level 2: Meeting)

- **Independent Sort:** 12 classification cards with self-check
- **Standard Recording:** Biodiversity investigation sheet
- **Group Discussion:** Participate in food web activity
- **Writing Task:** Complete exit ticket independently

### Extension (Level 3: Exceeding)

- **Advanced Research:** Investigate a local endangered species
- **Simulation Design:** Create their own ecosystem with 10+ species
- **Leadership Role:** Be "ecosystem expert" helping others at stations
- **Real-World Connection:** Research a real biodiversity crisis and propose solutions
- **Coding Extension:** Modify the p5.js simulation to add new species

| Criteria | Beginning (1) | Developing (2) | Meeting (3) | Exceeding (4) |
|----------|---------------|----------------|-------------|---------------|

| **Systems Thinking** | Sees organisms as isolated | Recognizes some connections | Explains interdependence | Predicts cascading effects |
| **Action Plan** | No plan or unrealistic | Vague plan | Specific actions | Specific, actionable, innovative |

## 7. Systems Literacy Connection

### The Core Message: "You Are Not a Lone Creature"

This lesson embodies the Systems Literacy vision through:

**1. Interdependence as Reality**
> "A lot of people experience obligation as 'why should I care?' That reaction is not because people are evil. It is because the system they are inside is invisible to them." — Systems Literacy Vision

Students discover that their food, air, and survival depend on billions of other living things—not as a moral lesson, but as an *engineering reality*.

**2. Feedback Loops in Ecosystems**
- Producers → Consumers → Decomposers → Producers (the cycle continues)
- More biodiversity = stronger system = more resilience
- Remove one piece = system weakens

**3. Code Connection**
Later in the unit, students will see this same pattern in code:
- Functions call other functions (like organisms depend on each other)
- Removing a function breaks the program (like removing a species breaks the ecosystem)
- Good coders, like good ecologists, plan for resilience

**4. From Abstract to Personal**
The lesson connects global biodiversity to:
- The food on their plate (farmers need pollinators!)
- The air they breathe (trees and plankton produce oxygen!)
- Their own bodies (millions of bacteria keeping us healthy!)

## 8. First Peoples Principles of Learning

### Integration Throughout

| Principle | How It's Applied |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Learning is holistic, reflexive, reflective, experiential, and relational** | Students experience biodiversity through hands-on activities, reflection (journals), and connections to their own lives |
| **Learning involves patience and time** | Food web activity demonstrates that ecosystems take time to develop; we can't rush nature |
| **Learning requires exploration of one's identity** | Students reflect on their own connection to ecosystems through journal prompts |
| **Learning is embedded in memory, history, and story** | Include Indigenous stories about interconnectedness (see Teacher Notes) |
| **Learning recognizes the role of indigenous knowledge** | Highlight how Indigenous peoples have maintained biodiversity for thousands of years through practices like controlled burning, seed keeping, and seasonal harvesting |

### Indigenous Knowledge Connections

- **Two-Eyed Seeing:** Combining Western scientific classification with Indigenous ways of knowing
- **Traditional Foods:** Connecting to local Indigenous food systems and biodiversity
- **Place-Based Learning:** Using local species and ecosystems relevant to students' communities

## 11. Teacher Notes

### Preparation Tips
1. **Gather materials** a day before: cards, yarn, chart paper
2. **Prep classification cards:** Print and laminate if possible; store in envelopes by station
3. **Test the p5.js simulation** before class (see section 14)
4. **Consider allergies:** If using food pictures, ensure none show allergen foods prominently

### Timing Guide
- Hook: 5-7 min
- Part 1: 12 min
- Part 2: 15 min
- Part 3: 15 min
- Part 4: 8 min
- **Buffer:** 3 min for transitions

### Classroom Management
- Use a visible timer for station rotations
- Assign station roles (timer, materials manager, recorder)
- Establish noise expectations for each activity level
- Have a "questioning cube" or parking lot for questions during stations

### Indigenous Story Suggestions
> **The Web of Life (adapted from various Indigenous traditions):**
> "Long ago, the Animals held a council. The Spirit asked each animal to give something to help the world. The Beaver gave wood for dams. The Bee gave pollination. The Wolf gave balance by eating the weak. Each creature contributed something unique. Without one, the others would struggle. This is why we must respect all living things."

### Common Misconceptions to Address
1. "Only cute animals matter" → Emphasize decomposers and microorganisms
2. "More of one species is always better" → Explain invasive species
3. "Humans are separate from nature" → Connect to our food systems
4. "Biodiversity is only about faraway rainforests" → Bring it home to local context

### Differentiation Quick Reference
| Need | Strategy |
|------|----------|
| ESL/ELL | Visual vocabulary cards, partner support, translated glossary |
| IEP | Simplified sorting, extra time, oral responses accepted |
| Advanced | Research extension, peer teaching role |
| Behavior | Active participation role, movement breaks |

## 13. Parent Guide

### What We Learned Today

Dear Families,

Today in Science, we explored **Biodiversity**—the incredible variety of all living things on Earth!

### Key Concepts

- **Biodiversity** = All the different kinds of life in a place
- Every living thing has a job in the ecosystem:
  - **Producers** make food (plants!)
  - **Consumers** eat other living things (animals, including us!)
  - **Decomposers** break down dead things (fungi, bacteria!)

### The Big Idea

> **"You are not a lone creature..."**

Just like we need friends and family, all living things need each other! When we have LOTS of different species, the ecosystem is stronger—like a team where everyone has a special job.

### Conversation Starters

Ask your child:
- "What's one living thing in our backyard/neighborhood?"
- "Why do you think we need different kinds of plants and animals?"
- "What would happen if all the bees disappeared?"
- "How can our family help biodiversity?"

### Try This at Home

🌱 **Plant something** — even a small herb on a windowsill adds to biodiversity!

🔍 **Go on a nature walk** — count how many different plants, birds, or insects you see!

📚 **Read together** — books about ecosystems, food chains, or local wildlife!

### Vocabulary to Review

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Biodiversity | All the different kinds of living things |
| Producer | Makes food (plants) |
| Consumer | Eats other living things |
| Decomposer | Breaks down dead things |
| Ecosystem | Where living things interact with each other and their environment |

## 14. p5.js Ecosystem Simulation

### Simulation Overview

This interactive simulation lets students explore how adding or removing species affects an ecosystem.

**Access:** `simulations/grade3-biodiversity/index.html`

### How It Works

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    ECOSYSTEM SIMULATOR                      │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                             │
│   [Sun] ──→ [Grass] ──→ [Rabbit] ──→ [Fox]                 │
│              ↓            ↓            ↓                    │
│         [Flower]      [Snake]      [Eagle]                  │
│              ↓                                                 │
│         [Beetle] ──→ [Decomposer]                           │
│                                                             │
│   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│   │  Species Count: 8    Biodiversity Score: ████░░  │   │
│   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘   │
│                                                             │
│   [REMOVE SPECIES]  [ADD SPECIES]  [RESET]                 │
│                                                             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Student Interactions

1. **Click species** to learn about their role
2. **Remove species** and watch what happens to the food web
3. **Add new species** from a menu (with trade-offs)
4. **Track biodiversity score** as they experiment

### Key Learning Moments

- When a producer is removed, consumers decline
- When a predator is removed, prey populations explode
- Highest biodiversity = most stable ecosystem

### Code Preview (For Teacher Reference)

```javascript
// Simplified ecosystem model
let ecosystem = {
  producers: ['grass', 'flowers', 'trees'],
  consumers: ['rabbit', 'fox', 'snake', 'eagle'],
  decomposers: ['fungi', 'bacteria']
};

function addSpecies(name, type) {
  ecosystem[type].push(name);
  updateBiodiversityScore();
}

function removeSpecies(name) {
  // Find and remove from appropriate category
  // Then check for cascading effects
  checkFoodWeb(name);
}
```

### Extension Challenge

Advanced students can:
- Modify the code to add new species
- Create their own food webs
- Test different scenarios (climate change, invasive species)

## 15. Extensions

### A. Week-Long Exploration

| Day | Activity |
|-----|----------|
| **Monday** | Nature walk — document biodiversity in schoolyard |
| **Tuesday** | Research a local species (library or online) |
| **Wednesday** | Create biodiversity posters for school |
| **Thursday** | Ecosystem diorama building |
| **Friday** | Present action plans to class |

### B. Outdoor Extension: Schoolyard Bioblitz

1. Divide into teams
2. Each team surveys one area (garden, field, trees, pavement cracks)
3. Count and categorize species found
4. Create a class "Biodiversity Map"
5. Compare: Which area has most? Least? Why?

### C. Digital Extension: iNaturalist Junior

- Create class account on iNaturalist
- Students photograph and identify species in their neighborhood
- Contribute to real citizen science data

### D. Art Extension: Biodiversity Murals

- Collaborative class mural showing a local ecosystem
- Include at least 15 species
- Show connections with arrows or pathways
- Label with roles (producer/consumer/decomposer)

### E. Writing Extension: Biodiversity News

- Students write a "news article" about a biodiversity discovery
- Include: Who, What, Where, Why it matters
- Optional: Create a class "Nature Newspaper"

### F. Math Extension: Data Analysis

- Graph biodiversity data from different ecosystems
- Calculate: "If we have 20 birds and 5 types, what's the ratio?"
- Compare: Schoolyard vs. local park vs. rainforest (use provided data)

### G. Family Extension: Heritage Foods

- Students research a traditional food from their family's heritage
- Identify the plants/animals involved
- Discuss: How does this connect to biodiversity?
- Share with class (bring sample if possible!)

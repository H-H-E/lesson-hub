# Grade 1 Science: Light and Shadow
## A Systems Literacy Journey into Light

---

## 🌟 Systems Literacy Vision Connection

*"You are not a lone creature being bossed around by morality; you are a living process inside other living processes."*

This lesson embraces the Systems Literacy vision by helping Grade 1 students discover that **light is a system**—not something that exists in isolation, but something that interacts with objects, travels from sources, and creates patterns that connect our world. Students will explore how light moves, changes, and creates shadows that tell us about the position and shape of objects. Just as we are part of larger systems, light is part of a larger cosmic dance that connects the Sun to our eyes, creating the world we see.

---

## 📋 4 SMART Learning Objectives

**By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:**

1. **Identify** at least 3 sources of light (Sun, lamp, flashlight) and explain that light helps us see things that we cannot see in the dark.

2. **Demonstrate** how a shadow is made by placing an object between a light source and a surface, and describe how the shadow changes when the object or light moves.

3. **Predict** the shape of a shadow based on the shape of the object creating it (e.g., a round ball makes a round shadow).

4. **Explain** that we need light to see, and describe how living things (including humans) use light from the Sun to live and grow.

## 🌞 Engaging Hook: The Mystery of the Hidden Object (3-4 minutes)

### Setup (Before students enter):
- Darken the classroom slightly (close curtains/blinds partially)
- Place a familiar object (e.g., a favorite stuffed animal or teacher-made puppet) behind a screen or large book so it's hidden in shadow
- Have a flashlight ready

### The Mystery (Teacher speaks):

**"Class, I have a mystery object in our classroom. It's hiding in the shadows right now. No one can see it—but I promise it's there!"**

Slowly turn on the flashlight, creating a beam of light.

**"Watch what happens when I bring in the light..."**

Slowly move the light to reveal the object, creating a shadow that gives away its shape.

**"Ah! There it is! The light showed us something we couldn't see before."**

**"Here's the question: How did the light help us find the hidden object? What made the shadow appear?"**

Give students 30 seconds to think and share.

**"Today we're going to become Light Explorers. By the end of our lesson, you'll understand how light moves, how shadows are made, and why light is SO important for every living thing on Earth—including YOU!"**

*Transition signal: Ring the bell three times.*

## 🕐 Complete Lesson Sequence

### Total Time: 60 minutes

---

### PHASE 1: EXPLORE 🔍 (15 minutes)

**Timing: 0:00 - 15:00**

#### Activity 1.1: Shadow Hunt Around the Room (8 minutes)

**Teacher Instructions:**
1. Divide class into groups of 3-4.
2. Give each group a flashlight and 3-4 objects.
3. Explain: "Find places in the room where you can make shadows. Try pointing the light from different directions."

**Exploration Prompts:**
- "Can you make the shadow bigger? Smaller?"
- "What happens when you move the light closer? Farther?"
- "Can you make the shadow disappear?"

**Key Questions During Activity:**
- "What do you notice about your shadow?"
- "Does the shadow match the shape of your object?"

#### Activity 1.2: Whole Group Discovery (7 minutes)

**Teacher Instructions:**
1. Bring students to the carpet with their objects.
2. Hold up a round object (ball) and a square block.
3. Shine light to create shadows of each.

**Demonstration:**
- "Watch my ball. What shape is its shadow?"
- "Now watch my block. What shape is its shadow?"

**Key Insight:** "The shadow looks like the object! That's because light travels in straight lines and can't go through solid objects."

*Transition signal: Ring the bell twice.*

---

### PHASE 2: LEARN 📖 (15 minutes)

**Timing: 15:00 - 30:00**

#### Activity 2.1: Mini-Lesson - How Shadows Are Made (7 minutes)

**Teacher Instructions:**
1. Use a puppet or draw on chart paper to explain:

**"Here's the secret about shadows:"**

- "Light comes from a source—the Sun, a lamp, a flashlight."
- "Light travels in a straight line, like an arrow."
- "When light hits an object, it STOPs. The light can't go through."
- "The dark shape behind the object? That's a SHADOW!"

**Demonstrate with body:**
- Hold up your hand in the flashlight beam.
- "My hand stops the light. The light can't get past my hand, so behind my hand, it's dark. That's a shadow!"

#### Activity 2.2: The Sun - Our Biggest Light Source (8 minutes)

**Teacher Instructions:**
1. Show pictures of the Sun.
2. Explain: "The Sun is the biggest light source for Earth. Without the Sun, it would be dark everywhere!"

**Key Points:**
- "The Sun gives us light during the day."
- "Plants use sunlight to make food—this is how they grow."
- "Animals need sunlight too—they use it to see, to find food, to stay warm."
- "Even WE need sunlight! Our bodies make Vitamin D when we're in the sunshine."

**Quick Think-Pair-Share:**
- "Turn to a partner and tell them: What do YOU use light for?"

*Transition signal: Ring the bell three times.*

---

### PHASE 3: CREATE 🎨 (20 minutes)

**Timing: 30:00 - 50:00**

#### Activity 3.1: Shadow Puppet Show Prep (10 minutes)

**Teacher Instructions:**
1. Explain: "You're going to create your own shadow show!"
2. Give each student a "Shadow Character" (template or let them cut simple shapes from black paper)
3. Demonstrate how to make the puppet stand using a popsicle stick or straw

**Creation Options:**
- Animals (fish, bird, cat)
- People
- Simple shapes
- Letters of their name

#### Activity 3.2: Shadow Puppet Performances (10 minutes)

**Teacher Instructions:**
1. Set up a "stage" using a white sheet or wall.
2. Darken the area with desks or use a partition.
3. Students take turns showing their puppets and making shadows.

**Sharing Prompt:**
- "What shape is your shadow?"
- "Did the shadow match your puppet?"

*Transition signal: Ring the bell once.*

---

### PHASE 4: REFLECT 🎤 (10 minutes)

**Timing: 50:00 - 60:00**

#### Activity 4.1: Circle Time Review (5 minutes)

**Gather students on the carpet.**

**Review Questions:**
1. "Where does light come from?" (Sources: Sun, lamps, flashlights)
2. "How is a shadow made?" (Light is blocked by an object)
3. "Does the shadow match the object?" (Yes!)
4. "Why is light important for living things?" (Helps us see, helps plants grow, gives us warmth)

#### Activity 4.2: Exit Ticket - One Thing I Learned (5 minutes)

**Instructions:**
- "On your paper, draw ONE thing you learned about light or shadows today."
- "Write or tell me one word that describes light."

## 🔗 Systems Literacy Connection

*"You are not a lone creature..."*

This lesson connects to Systems Literacy by helping students understand:

1. **Light is a system** — Light doesn't exist alone; it travels from sources (Sun, bulbs) through space, interacts with objects, and reaches our eyes. It's a flow, not a thing.

2. **We are part of the light system** — Our eyes receive light; our bodies use sunlight to make vitamin D; plants use light to make food (which we eat!). We are literally receiving the Sun's energy.

3. **Shadows show connections** — A shadow proves that light was there, even when we can't see it directly. Shadows tell us about things we can't see—they're evidence of invisible processes.

4. **Living things depend on light** — Plants → Sunlight → Food → Animals → Humans. This is a system! Without light, the whole system stops.

**Discussion Prompt (age-appropriate):**
- "The Sun is VERY far away—93 million miles! But its light reaches us and gives us warmth. How are we connected to something so far away?"

## 🌍 First Peoples Principles of Learning

### Integration Throughout Lesson:

**1. Learning is holistic, reflexive, reflective, experiential, and relational (focusing on relationships, not just facts):**
- Connect shadows to the natural world: "Look at the shadow of a tree—Indigenous peoples read shadows to understand time of day, season, and direction."
- Emphasize relationship: "The Sun gives us light every day. How do we take care of this gift?"

**2. Learning involves patience and time:**
- Allow unhurried exploration during Shadow Hunt—don't rush discoveries
- Honor different paces of learning

**3. Learning requires exploration of one’s identity:**
- "What shadow do you make? You're unique—no one else makes exactly YOUR shadow!"

**4. Learning is embedded in story:**
- Share: "Long ago, in many cultures, people told time by watching shadows. The shadow was like a clock!"
- Optional: Share a picture book about shadows or light (see Teacher Notes)

**5. Learning recognizes the role of ceremony:**
- Begin with intention: "Let's think carefully today, like we're doing something important."

### Indigenous Perspectives on Light and Shadows:

- Many First Peoples observe the Sun's position to determine seasons and hunting times
- Shadows were used to tell time before clocks existed
- The Sun is often seen as a relative or elder in many Indigenous worldviews
- Light ceremonies exist in many cultures—honoring the return of light after darkness

## 📝 Teacher Notes

### Lesson Preparation:
- Test all flashlights before class—have backup batteries ready
- If possible, do this lesson near a window for natural light comparison
- Prepare Shadow Puppet templates ahead of time (simple shapes)
- Create exit ticket papers with space for drawing

### Timing Adjustments:
- If class is shorter (45 min): Combine phases, reduce exploration time
- If class is longer (90 min): Add more exploration time, include p5.js simulation

### Classroom Management:
- Designate a "light corner" with flashlights to reduce congestion
- Use a signal (hand raise) for when students need help with flashlight
- Have a "shadow museum" area where students can display their work

### Special Considerations:
- **Eye safety:** Never look directly at the Sun; warn students not to point flashlights at eyes
- **Sensory sensitivity:** Some students may be uncomfortable in darkening—have option to sit near the door
- **Fine motor:** Provide chunky scissors and pre-cut options

### Discussion Starters:
- "Why do you think scientists study light?"
- "What's the most interesting shadow you've ever seen?"
- "If you could be any shadow, what would you be?"

### Common Misconceptions to Address:
- "Shadows are things" → Clarify: Shadows are the ABSENCE of light, not objects
- "Shadows move on their own" → Clarify: Shadows move because the light or object moves
- "Light stops at the object" → Clarify: Light is blocked; we can talk about "blocking" the light

## 👨‍👩‍👧 Parent Guide - Light and Shadow at Home

### What We Learned Today:

Your child explored light and shadows in science class! We discovered that:

- **Light comes from many places** — The Sun, lamps, flashlights, and even fire!
- **Shadows are made when something blocks light** — When light can't pass through an object, a shadow appears behind it.
- **Shadows match the shape of the object** — A round ball makes a round shadow!
- **Light is super important** — Plants need light to grow, and so do we!

### Fun Questions to Ask Your Child:

1. "Can you show me how to make a shadow?"
2. "What happens to your shadow when you move?"
3. "Where does the light in our house come from?"
4. "Can you make a shadow that looks like an animal?"

### Try This at Home! 🌟

**Shadow Play (5-10 minutes):**
- Turn off some lights and use a flashlight
- Make shadow puppets on the wall!
- Try to make your shadow big, then small

**Shadow Hunt (while walking):**
- Point out shadows you see: "Look, the tree has a shadow!"
- Ask: "Which direction is the shadow? Where is the light coming from?"

**Nighttime Exploration:**
- Turn on a lamp and ask: "Where is the shadow?"
- Move around the lamp and watch the shadow move!

- *The Shadow* by Brigitte B. Labbe

## 💻 p5.js Simulation - Light and Shadow Explorer

### Simulation Title: "Shadow Dance"

### Code (copy and paste into editor.p5js.org):

```javascript
// Shadow Dance - Grade 1 Light and Shadow Simulation
// Press and drag to move the object
// Press keys 1-3 to change light position

let objectX, objectY;
let lightX, lightY;
let objectSize = 60;
let lightMode = 1;

function setup() {
  createCanvas(600, 400);
  objectX = width / 2;
  objectY = height / 2;
  lightX = 100;
  lightY = 100;
  noStroke();
}

function draw() {
  // Sky background
  background(135, 206, 235);
  
  // Ground
  fill(139, 69, 19);
  rect(0, 300, width, 100);
  
  // Draw ground line
  stroke(100, 50, 0);
  line(0, 300, width, 300);
  noStroke();
  
  // Draw sun/light source
  fill(255, 215, 0);
  circle(lightX, lightY, 60);
  
  // Sun rays
  stroke(255, 215, 0, 100);
  strokeWeight(3);
  for (let i = 0; i < 8; i++) {
    let angle = i * PI / 4;
    line(lightX + cos(angle) * 40, lightY + sin(angle) * 40,
         lightX + cos(angle) * 50, lightY + sin(angle) * 50);
  }
  noStroke();
  
  // Draw the object
  fill(100, 50, 0);
  rectMode(CENTER);
  rect(objectX, objectY, objectSize, objectSize, 5);
  
  // Draw shadow on ground
  fill(50, 50, 50, 150);
  let shadowX = objectX + (lightX - objectX) * 1.5;
  let shadowY = 300;
  let shadowWidth = objectSize * (1 + abs(lightX - objectX) / 200);
  rect(shadowX, shadowY - 10, shadowWidth, 20, 5);
  
  // Instructions text
  fill(0);
  noStroke();
  textSize(16);
  text("Drag the square to move it!", 20, 30);
  text("Press 1, 2, or 3 to move the sun", 20, 55);
  text("Light Position: " + ["Left", "Center", "Right"][lightMode-1], 20, 80);
}

function mouseDragged() {
  objectX = mouseX;
  objectY = constrain(mouseY, 0, 280);
}

function keyPressed() {
  if (key === '1') {
    lightX = 100;
    lightY = 100;
    lightMode = 1;
  } else if (key === '2') {
    lightX = 300;
    lightY = 80;
    lightMode = 2;
  } else if (key === '3') {
    lightX = 500;
    lightY = 100;
    lightMode = 3;
  }
}
```

### How to Use in Class:

1. **Demonstration Mode (5 min):** Project the simulation and let students call out where to move the object
2. **Exploration Mode (10 min):** Students explore in pairs on tablets/laptops
3. **Prediction Challenge:** "What will happen if I move the light here?" → Students predict → Test it!

### Learning Goals with Simulation:
- See that shadows move when the object moves
- Observe that shadows change direction when light changes position
- Discover that the shadow always goes opposite the light

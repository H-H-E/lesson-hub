# Grade 1 Science: Living Things - First Peoples Principles of Learning Integration

## Overview

This document integrates Indigenous knowledge and First Peoples Principles of Learning into the Grade 1 Science unit on Living Things. These connections honour the wisdom of Indigenous peoples while enriching students' understanding of the natural world.

---

## Connection 1: Learning is Holistic (Whole Body + Heart + Mind)

**Principle:** Learning engages the whole person—thinking, feeling, sensing, and doing.

**Integration:**
- **Activity:** "Plants and Animals in Our Place" — Students go outside to observe living things in their schoolyard or neighbourhood. They use all their senses (look, listen, smell, feel) and share what they notice using sentences like "I see a bird. It makes me feel happy."
- **Why it matters:** This respects Indigenous knowledge that understanding comes from relationship with place, not just textbooks.

---

## Connection 2: Learning is Embedded in Story and Place

**Principle:** Learning is connected to land, history, and community stories.

**Integration:**
- **Activity:** "Elder or Family Story" — Students bring a story from home about a plant or animal (e.g., "My grandma says the cedar tree is medicine"). Teacher reads a picture book by an Indigenous author about local living things.
- **Example connection:** The Indigenous concept that animals teach us things (e.g., rabbit shows us patience, eagle shows us vision) connects to comparing animal characteristics.

---

## Connection 3: Learning Involves Reciprocity and Responsibility

**Principle:** We learn by giving back and caring for the land and each other.

**Integration:**
- **Activity:** "Caring for Our Classroom Garden" — Students plant seeds and take turns watering them. They learn that living things need care, and we have a responsibility to look after them.
- **Connection to content:** This directly supports the unit objective that living things need food, water, and air—and shows students they can be caretakers.

---

## Connection 4: Learning Recognizes All Knowledge Systems

**Principle:** Indigenous knowledge is a valid and important way of knowing.

**Integration:**
- **Activity:** "Two-Eyed Seeing" — When learning about animal needs, the teacher shares both Western science (animals need food, water, shelter) and Indigenous knowledge (animals have spirits, relationships, roles in the community).
- **Example:** When studying growth cycles, learn that some Indigenous communities track seasons by animal life cycles (e.g., salmon return = spring).

---

## Connection 5: Learning is Identity-Based and Place-Based

**Principle:** Learning is connected to who we are and where we come from.

**Integration:**
- **Activity:** "Living Things in Our Community" — Students draw or photograph living things from their own neighbourhood or cultural context. "What living things do you see where you live? What ones are special to your family?"
- **Differentiation:** Students share in home language if desired;honour diverse relationships with land.

---

## Teacher Notes

- **Language:** Use respectful, inclusive language when discussing Indigenous knowledge. Say "Indigenous peoples" rather than "Indians."
- **Consultation:** Where possible, invite Indigenous community members, elders, or knowledge keepers to share with the class.
- **Place-based:** Adapt activities to reflect the local Indigenous territory and community. Acknowledge the traditional lands your school sits on.
- **Safe space:** Create space for students to share their own cultural relationships with plants and animals, whatever their background.

---

## Assessment Connection

These connections can be assessed through:
- Oral sharing (storytelling)
- Drawing and labelling
- Class discussion participation
- Reflection prompts like "What did I learn from nature today?"

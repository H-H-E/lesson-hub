# Grade 5 Science - Module 3
## Earth's Water Systems

**Duration:** 60 minutes (Lesson 1 of 5)
**Grade:** 5
**Curriculum Area:** Science - Earth Sciences

---

## Learning Objectives (SMART)

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. **Identify** the three states of water (solid, liquid, gas) and explain how water changes states with 85% accuracy
2. **Diagram** the water cycle showing evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection
3. **Explain** how water moves through the Earth system using the concept of watersheds with 80% accuracy
4. **Analyze** the impact of human activity on local water quality using provided data

---

## Lesson Sequence

### Opening (10 min)
**Hook - The Water Mystery:**
Show three containers:
- Ice cubes (solid water)
- Water in a cup (liquid water)
- Invisible water (steam/fog) from hot water

Ask: "How can this all be the same thing?"

*Teacher connects to Indigenous knowledge:* "Indigenous peoples across what is now called British Columbia understood water in deep and sacred ways. The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) consider water to be one of the four sacred elements, along with fire, earth, and air. The Nisga'a and other coastal peoples have deep connections to the rivers and oceans that sustain their communities."

**Quick Write:** "What do you already know about the water cycle?"

### Exploration (20 min)
**Demonstration: Creating a Mini Water Cycle**

Set up a clear container with warm water:
1. Cover with plastic wrap
2. Place ice cubes on top
3. Observe over 10 minutes

Students record observations in a t-chart:

| Time | What I See | What I Think Is Happening |
|------|------------|---------------------------|
| 2 min | | |
| 5 min | | |
| 10 min | | |

*Key vocabulary to introduce:*
- **Evaporation:** Water turns from liquid to gas (heat makes it happen)
- **Condensation:** Water vapor turns back to liquid (cooling makes it happen)
- **Precipitation:** Water falling as rain, snow, or hail
- **Collection:** Where water gathers (oceans, lakes, glaciers)

### Discovery (15 min)
**Interactive Water Cycle Model**

Using a large classroom diagram, students place labels and arrows:

**Step-by-Step Build:**
1. Sun heats ocean → evaporation (water vapor rises)
2. Water vapor cools in atmosphere → condensation (forms clouds)
3. Clouds get heavy → precipitation (rain/snow)
4. Water flows back → collection (rivers, oceans)

**Introduce Watersheds:**
- Show a map of local watershed or BC watersheds
- Explain: "A watershed is like a giant bathtub - all the water in an area flows to the lowest point"
- Point out local rivers: Fraser River, Columbia River, Skeena River

*First Peoples Connection:* "Many Indigenous communities located villages and settlements based on watersheds - they understood that water flows from mountains to the sea, and this guided where fish, wildlife, and people would gather."

### Create (10 min)
**Activity: Water Cycle Comic / Poster**

Students create a visual representation showing:
1. The water cycle in nature
2. At least 3 key processes (evaporation, condensation, precipitation)
3. One human impact on water

Must include labels with definitions.

### Check (5 min)
**Exit Ticket:**
1. Draw the water cycle from memory (with labels)
2. Explain why the ocean doesn't overflow (where does water go?)

## Differentiation

### Level 1 - Support
- Pre-labeled water cycle diagram for reference
- Vocabulary cards with pictures and simple definitions
- Sentence stems for written explanations
- Work with a partner for comic creation
- Simplified version with only 3 stages
- Allow verbal assessment

### Level 2 - Guided
- Provide partially completed diagram
- Offer choice of format (comic, poster, or labeled drawing)
- Include word bank
- Check in at each stage of creation
- Scaffold the watershed explanation

### Level 3 - Extension
- Research how climate change affects the water cycle
- Investigate local water quality issues
- Compare freshwater and marine ecosystems
- Research Indigenous water governance and rights
- Create a more detailed watershed map

---

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment
- **Mini Water Cycle Observation:** Check for scientific thinking in predictions
- **Diagram Accuracy:** Evaluate labeled water cycle
- **Comic/Poster:** Check for all required elements and accuracy
- **Exit Ticket:** Assess retention of key concepts

### Summative Assessment Options
- **Option A:** Water Cycle Project - Create a 3D model or detailed poster
- **Option B:** Water Systems Test - Written assessment with diagrams
- **Option C:** Watershed Investigation - Research local watershed and present findings

### Success Criteria
- [ ] Correctly identifies three states of water
- [ ] Accurately diagrams the water cycle with labels
- [ ] Explains each process (evaporation, condensation, precipitation)
- [ ] Understands the concept of watersheds

## First Peoples Integration

### Content Connections
- **Water as Sacred:** Water is central to many Indigenous spiritual practices - smudging, ceremonies, purification
- **Fishing Rights:** Many Indigenous communities have deep connections to rivers and oceans for fishing and food
- **Water Stewardship:** Indigenous communities have been water stewards for thousands of years

### Indigenous Knowledge Integration
1. **Coast Salish and Haida:** Relationship with Pacific Ocean and marine ecosystems
2. **Interior Nations:** Relationship with rivers like the Fraser, Columbia, and Thompson
3. **All Indigenous Nations:** Understanding that water flows through and connects all beings

### Local BC Connections
- Fraser River watershed - largest in BC
- Columbia River - significant to many Indigenous nations
- Pacific Coast - home to many coastal First Nations
- Groundwater and aquifer understanding in traditional territories

### Cultural Protocols
- Acknowledge water as a living entity in many Indigenous worldviews
- Discuss Indigenous water rights and the importance of protecting water
- Connect to current Indigenous-led water protection initiatives

## Extension Activities

### Experiment: Creating Clouds
Create clouds in a bottle to demonstrate condensation.

### Research Project: Local Watershed
Students research their local watershed and present on:
- What rivers/streams feed it
- What lives in it
- What threats it faces
- How Indigenous communities interact with it

### Writing Connection: Water Poem
Write a free verse poem from water's perspective traveling through the water cycle.

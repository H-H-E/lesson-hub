# Grade 5 Science - Module 4
## Matter and Its Properties

**Duration:** 60 minutes (Lesson 1 of 5)
**Grade:** 5
**Curriculum Area:** Science - Physical Sciences

---

## Learning Objectives (SMART)

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. **Classify** matter into three states (solid, liquid, gas) based on observable properties with 90% accuracy
2. **Explain** the particle theory of matter - that all matter is made of tiny particles in motion
3. **Demonstrate** how matter changes state through heating or cooling with 80% accuracy
4. **Design** a fair test to compare properties of different materials (hardness, flexibility, transparency)

---

## Lesson Sequence

### Opening (10 min)
**Hook - The Disappearing Cup:**
Show a Styrofoam cup, a plastic cup, and a paper cup. Ask:
- "If I put hot water in each, what will happen?"
- "Which will melt first? Why?"

Let students make predictions, then show a time-lapse video or demonstrate with heat lamps.

*Teacher connects to Indigenous knowledge:* "Indigenous peoples have always understood that matter changes form. The Secwepemc (Shuswap) and other Nations understood that materials from the land - stone, wood, plant fibers - have different properties and uses. Traditional technologies, like making baskets from cedar bark or using obsidian for tools, show deep understanding of matter."

**Quick Vocabulary Review:** solid, liquid, gas, matter

### Exploration (20 min)
**Station Lab: Properties of Matter**

Students rotate through 5 stations in 4-minute blocks:

**Station 1: Shape and Volume**
- Test different containers with water and solids
- Question: Does a solid always keep its shape? Does a liquid always take the container's shape?

**Station 2: Hardness Test**
- Scratch test with various materials
- Use Mohs hardness scale reference
- Materials: wood, plastic, metal, rock samples

**Station 3: Flexibility**
- Bend different materials
- Record which bend, which break, which stretch

**Station 4: Transparency**
- Test materials: clear plastic, frosted glass, cardboard, fabric
- Sort into transparent, translucent, opaque

**Station 5: State Changes**
- Observe ice melting (solid → liquid)
- Observe water evaporating (liquid → gas) with hot plate (teacher demo) or hand warmers

### Discovery (15 min)
**Particle Theory Introduction**

Use a ball-and-stick model or simple animation to explain:
- All matter is made of tiny particles
- Particles are always moving
- Heat affects how fast particles move

**Key Concepts:**
- Solids: particles packed tight, vibrate in place
- Liquids: particles loosely connected, can flow
- Gas: particles spread far apart, move fast

*First Peoples Connection:* "Indigenous stone tool technologies demonstrate understanding of matter - heating stone (flint, obsidian) changes its structure, making it easier to shape. This is similar to how we heat metals today to shape them."

### Create (10 min)
**Activity: Material Design Challenge**

Students become "material engineers" for a specific purpose:
- Design a material that could be used for: a winter jacket, a window, a bridge, a boat

Must include:
- What state(s) of matter it would be
- At least 3 properties it must have
- Why these properties make it suitable

### Check (5 min)
**Exit Ticket:**
1. Draw particles in a solid, liquid, and gas
2. Explain one way you could change a solid into a liquid

## Differentiation

### Level 1 - Support
- Simplified station recording sheets
- Visual vocabulary cards with images
- Partner work at all stations
- Sentence stems for explanations
- Pre-cut particles for model building
- Allow verbal responses

### Level 2 - Guided
- Provide recording sheet prompts
- Offer choice of design challenge
- Include word bank
- Check in between stations
- Allow collaboration

### Level 3 - Extension
- Research the particle theory at the molecular level
- Investigate plasma as a fourth state of matter
- Research traditional Indigenous material technologies
- Create a more complex material design with detailed specifications

---

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment
- **Station Observations:** Checklist for engagement and scientific thinking
- **Recording Sheets:** Check for completion and accuracy
- **Design Challenge:** Evaluate properties and reasoning
- **Exit Ticket:** Quick assessment of particle theory understanding

### Summative Assessment Options
- **Option A:** Matter Investigation Book - Document all experiments with illustrations
- **Option B:** Matter Test - Written assessment with diagrams and explanations
- **Option C:** Material Invention - Create a new material with properties and present to class

### Success Criteria
- [ ] Correctly classifies matter into three states
- [ ] Explains particle theory accurately
- [ ] Demonstrates understanding of state changes
- [ ] Tests and compares material properties

## First Peoples Integration

### Content Connections
- **Traditional Materials:** Cedar bark, pine needles, animal hides, stone, bone, shell
- **Traditional Technologies:** Understanding matter properties in tools, housing, clothing
- **Processing Materials:** Smoking fish, drying meat, preparing plant fibers

### Indigenous Knowledge Integration
1. **Coast Salish:** Cedar - understanding its properties for weaving, building, medicine
2. **Interior Nations:** Using stone with specific properties for tools
3. **All Nations:** Processing materials through heating, drying, combining

### Local BC Examples
- **Cedar:** The "tree of life" - used for canoes, longhouses, clothing, baskets
- **Obsidian:** Volcanic glass used for arrowheads and tools
- **Stone:** Various rocks used for grinding, cooking, building

### Connecting to Current Science
- Compare traditional material processing to modern materials science
- Discuss how Indigenous knowledge influenced modern understanding
- Connect to sustainable materials research

### Cultural Sensitivity
- Acknowledge the sophisticated science in traditional technologies
- Present Indigenous knowledge with respect - it's valid science
- Connect to living cultures and current practices

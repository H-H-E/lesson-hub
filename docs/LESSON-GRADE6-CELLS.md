## 5. Teacher Background Knowledge

### The Cell Theory (4 Principles)
1. **All living things are made of cells** - Schwann & Schleiden (1838-39)
2. **Cells are the basic unit of life** - Rudolf Virchow (1855)
3. **All cells come from pre-existing cells** - Cell division
4. **Cells contain hereditary material** (DNA) - Modern understanding

### Key Differences: Plant vs. Animal Cells

| Feature | Plant Cell | Animal Cell |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Cell Wall | ✅ Present (cellulose) | ❌ Absent |
| Chloroplasts | ✅ Present | ❌ Absent |
| Vacuole | ✅ Large central vacuole | ✅ Small/several |
| Shape | Fixed, rectangular | Flexible, round |
| Centrioles | ✅ Present | ❌ Absent in most |

### Systems Thinking Connections
- Cells → Tissues → Organs → Organ Systems → Organism
- Each cell is both independent AND part of a larger system
- Cell failure = system failure (cancer, disease)

### Common Misconceptions
- "Cells are too small to see" → They CAN be seen with microscopes
- "Only plants have cells" → ALL living things have cells
- "Cells don't do anything" → They perform ALL life functions

## 6. Lesson Sequence

### Lesson 1: What Is a Cell? (45 min)

**Opening (10 min) - Hook**
> "If you could shrink down to the size of a grain of salt, what would you see inside your own hand?"

- Show 30-second video of cells moving
- Brainstorm: What do students already know about cells?
- Record on KWL chart (Know, Want to know, Learned)

**Direct Instruction (15 min)**
- Present: History of cell discovery (Hooke, Leeuwenhoek)
- Introduce: Cell theory - 4 principles
- Visual: Animated diagram of cells dividing

**Guided Practice (12 min)**
- Activity: "Cell Bingo" - Match terms to definitions
- Whole-class: Label a basic animal cell together

**Independent Practice (5 min)**
- Exit ticket: Write 3 things you learned about cells

**Closing (3 min)**
- Preview: "Tomorrow we look inside real cells!"
- Homework: Bring any questions about cells

---

### Lesson 2: Inside the Cell - Microscope Lab (45 min)

**Opening (5 min)**
- Review: Cell theory questions (cold call)
- Demonstrate: Proper microscope use

**Direct Instruction (10 min)**
- Model: How to prepare a wet mount slide
- Show: Diagram of onion cell vs. cheek cell

**Guided Practice (20 min) - ROTATION STATIONS**

| Station | Activity | Time |
|---------|----------|------|
| A | Observe onion epidermis cells | 7 min |
| B | Observe cheek (animal) cells | 7 min |
| C | Observe elodea (plant) cells | 7 min |
| D | Draw & label observed cells | 7 min |

**Independent Practice (8 min)**
- Complete: Cell comparison chart
- Write: 2 observations + 1 question

**Closing (2 min)**
- Share: "What surprised you most?"
- Collect: Observation journals

---

### Lesson 3: Plant vs. Animal Cells (45 min)

**Opening (5 min)**
- Quiz: Quick 5-question formative check
- Connect: Review yesterday's observations

**Direct Instruction (12 min)**
- Compare: Side-by-side Venn diagram
- Introduce: 4 new organelles (mitochondria, chloroplast, vacuole, cell wall)
- Analogy: "Cell = Mini City" - each organelle has a job

**Guided Practice (15 min)**
- Activity: "Cell Factory" - Assign roles
  - Nucleus = Mayor/Boss
  - Mitochondria = Power plant
  - Cell membrane = Security guard
  - Ribosomes = Factory workers
  - Golgi = Shipping department
  - Vacuole = Warehouse

**Independent Practice (10 min)**
- Create: Cell comparison graphic organizer
- Choose: Plant cell OR animal cell to draw in detail

**Closing (3 min)**
- Exit question: "Why do plant cells need a cell wall but animals don't?"
- Preview: "How do cells work TOGETHER?"

---

### Lesson 4: Cells as Systems (45 min)

**Opening (8 min)**
- Connection: "How is a soccer team like a body?" (Systems thinking)
- Introduce: Cells → Tissues → Organs → Systems → Organism

**Direct Instruction (12 min)**
- Video: "The Human Body - Cells at Work" (5 min)
- Discuss: How do cells specialize?
- Example: Nerve cells, blood cells, skin cells - SAME DNA, different jobs!

**Guided Practice (15 min)**
- Activity: "Build a Human" - Match cells to body systems
  - Muscle cells → Muscular system
  - Nerve cells → Nervous system
  - Blood cells → Circulatory system
  - Bone cells → Skeletal system
  - Skin cells → Integumentary system

**Independent Practice (8 min)**
- Create: Systems map showing cell → tissue → organ → system
- Reflection: "What happens when ONE part of a system fails?"

**Closing (2 min)**
- Discussion: Why is it important to study cells?
- Homework: Interview someone about a disease related to cells

---

### Lesson 5: Cell Model Project (45 min)

**Opening (5 min)**
- Review: All vocabulary (word wall)
- Present: Project criteria and rubric

**Direct Instruction (5 min)**
- Show: Examples of 3D cell models (student examples)
- Demonstrate: Materials and techniques

**Guided Practice (25 min) - WORK TIME**
- Create: 3D cell model (physical or digital)
- Include: At least 6 labeled structures
- Choice: Plant cell, animal cell, or both connected

**Independent Practice (8 min)**
- Peer feedback: "2 stars and a wish"
- Finalize: Labels and presentation

**Closing (2 min)**
- Gallery walk preview
- Self-assessment: Rate your understanding (1-4)

## 10. Safety Considerations

### Microscope Safety
- Always carry microscopes with two hands
- Never touch lenses with fingers
- Slide placement: centered, not sliding around
- Clean up broken slides immediately (tell teacher)

### Biological Samples
- No eating or drinking in lab
- Wash hands after handling specimens
- Dispose of slides properly (teacher collects)
- Cheek swab: Use sterile swabs, dispose properly

### General Lab Safety
- Keep workspace clean
- Report spills immediately
- No running in the classroom
- Listen for and follow all safety instructions

## 11. Teacher Notes

### Common Challenges
1. **Students can't focus microscope** → Practice with "letter e" slide first
2. **Confusing plant/animal cells** → Use color coding consistently
3. **Forgetting vocabulary** → Daily word wall review
4. **Rushing the model project** → Build in checkpoints

### Tips for Success
- Pre-teach microscope use BEFORE lab day
- Use "Cell City" analogy throughout unit
- Connect to students' bodies ("Your hand has TRILLIONS of cells!")
- Celebrate student questions - they drive inquiry
- Take photos of microscope views for students to reference

### Differentiation Reminders
- Always have 3 levels of materials ready
- Group strategically for peer support
- Check in with ELL students daily
- Offer multiple response formats

## 12. Systems Literacy Integration

### What is Systems Literacy?
The ability to understand how parts connect to form whole systems, recognize patterns, and see how changes in one part affect the whole.

### How It's Embedded in This Unit

**Big Ideas:**
- Cells are parts of larger systems (tissues → organs → systems)
- Each organelle has a specialized function contributing to the whole cell
- Cell health = Organ health = Organism health

**Thinking Routines:**
- **Part-Whole:** What parts make up this system? How do they work together?
- **Input-Output:** What goes into the cell? What comes out?
- **Feedback Loops:** How does the cell respond to its environment?
- **Emergence:** What new properties emerge when cells work together?

**Systems Questions Students Learn to Ask:**
1. What are the parts of this system?
2. How do the parts interact?
3. What does the whole system do that parts cannot do alone?
4. What happens if one part fails?
5. How is this system connected to other systems?

**Assessment of Systems Thinking:**
- Systems map creation
- "What if" scenarios (What if mitochondria stopped working?)
- Connecting cell function to body system function
